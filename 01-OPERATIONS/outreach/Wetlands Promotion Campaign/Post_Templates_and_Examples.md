## POST 2: The Tailings Challenge - 1.4 Trillion Reasons to Act
**Timing:** Week of November 13, 2025
**Theme:** Problem statement with statistics

### LinkedIn Post

Here's a number that keeps me up at night: 1,400,000,000,000 litres.

That's 1.4 trillion litres of oil sands process-affected water currently stored in tailings ponds across Alberta. To put that in perspective, it's roughly equivalent to 560,000 Olympic swimming pools, or enough water to cover the entire city of Calgary to a depth of about 2 metres.

And we've never been allowed to release a drop of it.

The culprit? Naphthenic acids—a complex family of organic compounds that make this water too toxic to return to the environment. These acids have accumulated over decades of mining operations, creating an environmental liability that grows larger every year.

The challenge isn't just the volume. It's the permanence. Without viable treatment solutions, these tailings ponds become perpetual features on the landscape—exactly the opposite of what responsible resource development should look like. At the end of mine life, operators need to return these lands to equivalent capability, and that's simply not possible with 300 square kilometres of toxic water sitting on the surface.

Traditional treatment approaches—chemical, thermal, or mechanical—struggle with the scale and cost. Processing 1.4 trillion litres using conventional methods would be economically prohibitive and energy-intensive.

This is why the GROW project's research on constructed treatment wetlands is so compelling. Semi-passive, cost-effective treatment that works with natural processes rather than fighting against them. It's not a silver bullet, but it might be a critical piece of the solution we've been missing.

The presentations this week from Dr. Jason Ahad and Dr. Graham van Aggelen dive into the genomics and analytical approaches being used to optimize these systems. I'm eager to see how the science translates to operational reality.

## POST 10: Biosensors Breakthrough - Lewenza Presentation Preview
**Timing:** Week of January 15, 2026 (ahead of March 5 presentation)
**Theme:** Technology spotlight on Luminous Bio Solutions

### LinkedIn Post

Imagine being able to detect toxic naphthenic acids in water within 24 hours using bacteria that literally light up in response.

On March 5th, Dr. Shawn Lewenza—Chief Science Officer of Luminous Bio Solutions and researcher at Athabasca University and University of Calgary—will present breakthrough biosensor technology to the GROW seminar series. This is the work I've been most excited to share.

Here's why this matters: Monitoring is the bottleneck in wetland optimization. Traditional analytical methods for naphthenic acids are expensive, slow, and require sophisticated equipment like orbitrap mass spectrometry. When you're trying to optimize a treatment system in real-time, waiting days or weeks for lab results isn't viable.

Our bacterial biosensors solve this problem elegantly. These engineered bacteria directly sense naphthenic acids and respond by producing light—luminescence that's proportional to the concentration of toxins present. We've developed a panel of biosensors, each responding to a unique subset of naphthenic acids within the complex mixtures found in tailings.

The qualitative method detects NAs within 24 hours. The quantitative method, using solid phase extraction, provides concentration estimates (mg/L) that we've validated against mass spectrometry in both mesocosm and constructed wetland treatment systems.

This is high-throughput monitoring technology that could finally make large-scale wetland remediation operationally feasible. Instead of sending hundreds of samples to expensive labs, operators could run rapid on-site tests, adjust wetland parameters, and track treatment progress in near real-time.

Simple. Rapid. Specific. Quantitative. And critically—scalable to the size of the challenge we're facing.

If you're interested in the intersection of synthetic biology and environmental remediation, mark March 5th at 11 AM MT on your calendar. This presentation will showcase how biological detection methods are changing the game for tailings management.

## POST 21: Series Conclusion - Path Forward for Industry
**Timing:** Week of April 2, 2026
**Theme:** Series wrap-up + call to action

### LinkedIn Post

Five months. Seventeen presentations. One compelling conclusion: Nature-based solutions for oil sands tailings remediation aren't just promising—they're becoming operationally viable.

The GROW seminar series concluded this week, and I want to share what I'm taking away from a business perspective:

**The Science Is Solid**
From genomics-based wetland optimization to validated biosensor monitoring to demonstrated naphthenic acid biodegradation—the research shows constructed treatment wetlands can handle the complexity of oil sands process-affected water.

**The Economics Make Sense**
Semi-passive treatment systems operate at a fraction of the cost of conventional thermal, chemical, or mechanical approaches. When you're dealing with 1.4 trillion litres, cost-per-litre matters enormously.

**The Path Forward Is Clear**
This isn't about choosing between wetlands and other technologies—it's about integration. Wetlands can be part of a treatment train, potentially in conjunction with biochar enhancement, targeted chemical treatment for specific contaminants, and advanced monitoring systems.

**But Implementation Requires Action**
Research doesn't remediate tailings. We need industry, government, and technology providers to move from pilot projects to commercial deployment. That means:

• Regulatory frameworks that accommodate innovative treatment approaches
• Collaboration between operators to share learnings and de-risk implementation
• Investment in monitoring infrastructure that enables optimization
• Engagement with Indigenous communities and stakeholders for social licence
• Continued funding for research that improves effectiveness and reduces costs

Through Luminous Bio Solutions' work on rapid biosensor monitoring, I've seen firsthand how small innovations in one area—like detection technology—can unlock possibilities across the entire system. That's the power of multidisciplinary collaboration.

Alberta has 300 square kilometres of tailings that need remediation. That's the challenge. But after following this seminar series, I genuinely believe we have the tools, the knowledge, and increasingly the economic case to start addressing it.

The question is: Do we have the collective will?

To the researchers at University of Calgary, Athabasca University, Brock University, University of Saskatchewan, SFU, and all the contributing institutions—thank you for advancing the science. To the operators, regulators, and decision-makers reading this—let's talk about what comes next.

The research has been done. Now it's time to deploy it.
