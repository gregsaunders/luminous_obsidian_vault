Abstract

The potential discharge of groundwater contaminated by oil sands process-affected water (OSPW) is a concern for aquatic ecosystems near tailings ponds. Groundwater in the area, but unaffected by OSPW, may contain similar compounds, complicating the assessment of potential ecological impacts. In this study, 177 shallow groundwater samples were collected from riparian areas along the Athabasca River and tributaries proximate to oil sands developments. For pond-site samples (71; adjacent to study tailings pond), Canadian aquatic life guidelines were exceeded for 11 of 20 assessed compounds. However, non-pond samples (54; not near any tailings pond) provided similar exceedances. Statistical analyses indicate that pond-site and non-pond samples were indistinguishable for all but seven parameters assessed, including salts, many trace metals, and fluorescence profiles of aromatic naphthenic acids (ANA). This suggests that, regarding the tested parameters, groundwater adjacent to the study tailings pond generally poses no greater ecological risk than other nearby groundwaters at this time. Multivariate analyses applied to the groundwater data set separated into 11 smaller zones support this conclusion, but show some variation between zones. Geological and potential OSPW influences could not be distinguished based on major ions and metals concentrations. However, similarities in indicator parameters, namely ANA, F, Mo, Se, and Na-Cl ratio, were noted between a small subset of samples from two pond-site zones and two OSPW samples and two shallow groundwater samples documented as likely OSPW affected. This indicator-based screening suggests that OSPW-affected groundwater may be reaching Athabasca River sediments at a few locations.

Introduction

Surface mining of oil sands requires a caustic hot water extraction for the isolation of bitumen (FTFC 1995), which results in the production of tailings, a waste product consisting of an alkaline and saline suspension of sand, silt, clay, residual bitumen, and naphtha. The aqueous portion of the waste, oil sands process-affected water (OSPW), contains a complex mixture of neutral and polar organic compounds, including naphthenic acids (NAs), which have demonstrated acute (Frank et al. 2008; Scarlett et al. 2013) and sublethal (Reinardy et al. 2013) toxicity, as well as elevated levels of many metals (Allen 2008) and major ions (i.e. Na, Cl, SO4, CO3). An estimated 840millionm3 of OSPW are contained within settling basins (i.e., tailings ponds) in the mining areas of northeastern Alberta, Canada (Energy Resources Conservation Board 2010).

Oil sands tailings ponds are generally constructed of hydraulically emplaced coarse tailings (largely sand) that form a containment dyke. Collection systems within the dyke are designed to capture internal drainage, which may contain OSPW, and pond leakage, while also providing geotechnical stability. Water captured is subsequently returned to the tailings ponds. Leakage can be restricted via the development of fine sediment layers of low hydraulic conductivity at the bottom of these ponds as the tailings settle (Ferguson et al. 2009). Still, there is public concern over OSPW leakage to groundwater and the potential transport of OSPW-affected groundwater to nearby surface waters, where it may affect aquatic ecosystems (Royal Society of Canada Expert Panel 2010). Indeed, several field-based studies have shown or suggested OSPW-affected groundwater flowing from various tailings ponds or their dykes (Hunter 2001; MacKinnon et al. 2005; Ross et al. 2012; Yasuda et al. 2010), including one documented plume extending approximately 500m (Oiffer et al. 2009). Numerical modeling of another (now reclaimed) tailings pond directly adjacent to the Athabasca River suggested that leakage from the pond and dyke system to underlying aquifer materials was possible (Ferguson et al. 2009). Total leakage was estimated for 1999 conditions at less than 65L/s, which is about 0.1% of the lowest daily Athabasca River flow recorded, with direct loss from the bottom of the pond estimated at about 2.0L/s. Indications of OSPW-affected groundwater reaching shallow sediments of the Athabasca River were reported by Hunter (2001), based largely on NA and Na concentrations and Na-Cl ratios. Subsequently, recent work (Frank et al. 2014) using a combination of newly developed advanced separation and high-resolution analytical techniques provided the strongest indication to date of OSPW-affected groundwater (two samples) reaching the sediments of the Athabasca River beside this same tailings pond. However, it did not investigate the extent and potential environmental risks posed by any probable discharge into surface waters.

Groundwaters in the area of oil sands development, but unaffected by tailings pond leakage, may contain similar mixtures of potentially toxic compounds (i.e., metals, salts, neutral and polar organics) to those found in OSPW. Some of these compounds may be derived from atmospheric inputs (e.g., upgrader emissions, open-pit mine erosion) affecting recharge water. Others may be acquired along the groundwater flow path, with possible inputs from several geological formations, including one (the McMurray formation) containing natural oil sands. This complicates the identification of OSPW-affected groundwater and the assessment of potential ecological impacts from tailings pond leakage.

In this study, the water quality of shallow riparian groundwater along the entire length (3km) of the same reclaimed tailings pond studied previously (Frank et al. 2014) (i.e., where any OSPW-affected groundwater originating from the pond would ultimately be expected to discharge) was investigated. These samples were compared to those collected from areas directly upstream and downstream of the pond, directly opposite on the Athabasca River, and on nearby tributaries (Ells, Muskeg, and Steepbank Rivers). Most of these comparison areas are not expected to be affected by leakage from this or any other tailings ponds, but many are within the general area of oil sands development (i.e., not many of them are considered pristine background). Unlike the previous study (Frank et al. 2014), which used advanced analytical techniques on seven shallow groundwater samples (among other samples potentially affected by OSPW), this work incorporated a larger number of such samples (177 total) that were analyzed for a wide range of standard geochemical parameters, including major ions, metals, and metalloids (as in Kelly et al. 2010) and aromatic naphthenic acids (ANA) detected by synchronous fluorescence spectroscopy (SFS) (Kavanagh et al. 2009). Oil sands aromatic acid mixtures have been consistently found with the acid extractable organic fraction of industrial and natural bitumen-impacted waters (Frank et al. 2014; Kavanagh et al. 2009; Rowland et al. 2011), and this fraction has long been associated with the toxicity of OSPW (Clemente and Fedorak 2005; MacKinnon and Boerger 1986; Reinardy et al. 2013; Scarlett et al. 2013).

The primary objective of this study was to assess whether shallow groundwater conditions adjacent to the tailings pond (one of the first ponds created in the area and closest to the Athabasca River) posed a greater toxicity risk than nearby reference sites, with the hypothesis that a higher risk profile adjacent to the pond would be associated with OSPW contamination. This was accomplished (Assessment 1 in Table 1) by comparing concentrations of geochemical constituents (or proxies) in groundwater from riparian areas adjacent to the study tailings pond (i.e., pond-site samples) and those far from any tailings pond (i.e., non-pond samples). It is not expected that these shallow riparian groundwater samples would be affected by tailings ponds several kilometers away, because any plumes emanating from these would likely be pushed deeper by recharging water from the intervening area and would likely have travel times too long to have reached the rivers at this time, as is the case for the plume studied by Oiffer et al. (2009). Any influence of sampling depth between these two groups was considered in Assessment 2. Risk profiles for geochemicals were generated by comparing their concentrations to their respective Canadian Guidelines for the Protection of Freshwater Aquatic Life (CCME 2007), consistent with previous approaches (Conant et al. 2004; Roy and Bickerton 2012) and recommended practice in federal guidance documents (FCSAP Secretariat of Environment Canada 2014; USEPA 2008). The sampling conducted in this study is highly relevant to the protection of aquatic life given the importance of the exchange between shallow riparian groundwater and surface waters within the hyporheic zone, especially for burrowing benthic organisms (Lawrence et al. 2013; Rempel et al. 2000). However, this study provides no information on discharge from deeper groundwater flow paths to the middle of the rivers. Also, these results may not apply to winter periods when river stage and flow are much lower (Figure S1), because groundwater discharge conditions and locations may be substantially different.

A secondary objective was to determine whether any combinations of standard geochemistry metrics (Assessments 3 and 4 in Table 1) could be applied to cost-effectively screen large sets of groundwater samples for more advanced analytical (i.e., Level 2 [Frank et al. 2014]) confirmation of OSPW contamination. Many of these parameters have shown promise for site-specific investigations (i.e., tracking known plumes) (Oiffer et al. 2009) and have been proposed as screening parameters previously (Baker 1999; Hunter 2001; Kavanagh et al. 2009). Here the authors provide a more thorough assessment, given this large data set and that six of these samples have previously been characterized as either affected or not affected by OSPW, using high-level analytical techniques (Frank et al. 2014). Comparisons are also made to recently collected OSPW samples from two different tailings ponds, given the reclaimed pond is now dry. Though these likely differ in water chemistry from the OSPW originally in the study pond, which itself likely varied through time, together these are deemed a reasonable (and best-available) representation.

Research Method

Groundwater Sampling and Analyses

Shallow groundwater samples were collected at depths of 30 to 120 cm below the streambed along the edge (banks or connected sandbars) of the Athabasca River and associated tributaries (Ells River, Muskeg River, Steepbank River; Figure 1a; Table S3), in 2009 (24 samples), 2010 (71 samples), and 2011 (84 samples). Coordinates of sampling locations (Table S6 and kml file for easy plotting) are provided in the Supporting Information (Appendix S6). Note that this data set includes five of the six drive-point samples assessed for OSPW-influence by Frank et al. (2014) (excluding Drive-point 2, due to incomplete geochemistry). For comparison purposes the sampling areas have been differentiated into spatial zones (Figure 1). Four of these zones (PA, PB, PC, and PD) are adjacent to the study pond, and together are considered the pond-site samples for Assessment 1 (Table 1). The non-pond samples for Assessment 1 consist of zones EL, SB, NE, NW, and SW; none are directly adjacent to any tailings pond. Each of the remaining zones (MR, EP) contained sample locations that were adjacent to some other tailings pond. All zones are considered in Assessments 24 (Table 1). Assignment of zones along the Athabasca River was based on an assumption that the shallow groundwater generally flowed perpendicular to the river. Potential flow northward and southward from the study pond into adjacent zones is considered in the assessment of the results. Sample locations were not evenly distributed temporally (but limited to May to September period) or spatially, yet they generally cover a broad area within each zone. In five cases, samples were collected at the same basic location (<5m separation) in different years. Multiple samples at a single location were collected sequentially at two (22 locations) or three (5 locations) different depths. Fifteen of these multiple-depth sets were collected within a small area (750 m2) in zone PB; all but one of these samples were excluded from the broad-scale comparative analyses (to avoid bias from this subset of samples collected over such a small scale relative to the majority of other samples), but they were all included in the sampling depth analysis.

Samples were obtained with a peristaltic pump using a stainless steel drive-point system (Roy and Bickerton 2010). Several water properties (pH, electrical conductivity [EC], dissolved oxygen [DO], temperature) were monitored on site with hand-held meters. If the groundwater had similar properties to the above river water, possibly indicating shallow hyporheic flow or short-circuiting along the rod, the sampler was driven deeper. In some cases, the penetration depth was limited by competent bedrock (likely upper Devonian carbonates in most cases, which underlies the McMurray formation [King and Yetter 2011]). Sample handling and storage details are provided in Appendix S2.

The Athabasca River and these tributary rivers occur in incised valleys and are the primary groundwater drainage features in the area (King and Yetter 2011). Many studies have provided evidence of groundwater flow to these rivers or groundwater discharge at the rivers (e.g., Ferguson et al. 2009; Gibson et al. 2013; Oiffer et al. 2009). However, it is possible that the shallow groundwater samples of this study, even under discharge conditions, could be influenced by surface water, by (1) short-circuiting while sampling, (2) hyporheic flows across meanders or induced by streambed topography, or (3) bank inflow or storage associated with fluctuating river levels or nearby groundwater pumping. The former can be dealt with rather easily in the field (noted above); however, it can be difficult to identify influences of the latter two processes (Cranswick and Cook 2015). To the best of our knowledge, there was no substantial groundwater pumping near the areas sampled. An evaluation of our sampling data (Appendix S3) suggests that these remaining influences are not substantial for the vast majority of samples, though no definitive determination can be made. Briefly, attempts were made to sample during low flow or steady river level periods, to reduce impacts from bank storage, though this was not possible for all sampling events (<27% of samples). Also, differences in water quality parameters (focused on EC, but including DO, pH and a principle component analysis [PCA] of major ions and metals) between the groundwater samples and river water were predominantly quite marked. Very few showed strongly suggestive evidence of river water influence (though none seemed mostly river water), with most of these collected during higher river discharge. Despite the potential for these few samples to be partly influenced by infiltrated river water, none were excluded from the data set, because there was no definitive influence proven and no clear cut-off criteria for exclusion. This inclusion is not expected to alter the general statistical results, because (1) the number of affected samples appears to be very small, (2) their concentrations will only be slightly lowered (dilution with river water), and (3) they encompass samples from multiple zones, including pond-site and non-pond groups. Implications of potential river water influences on the groundwater data are considered in the discussion of results.

Chemical Analyses

The analysis of major ions was performed by ion chromatography for anions and by direct aspiration using an inductively coupled argon plasma system for cations (Roy and Bickerton 2010). Trace metals were analyzed at Environment Canada's National Laboratory for Environmental Testing (NLET) (Burlington, ON) using inductively coupled plasma-sector field mass spectrometry (NLET 2008). For 20102011 samples, ammonium was analyzed by spectrophotometry using a phenolhypochlorite reagent (absorbance at 640 nm), while a Perkin-Elmer Luminescence spectrometer LS50B was used for SFS analysis, with FL Winlab 3 software (Perkin-Elmer, Norwalk, Connecticut) used for data collection, as previously described (Kavanagh et al. 2009). The expected SFS maxima for an oil sands NA profile are at 282, 320, and 333nm (Kavanagh et al. 2009; Rowland et al. 2011). In all previous work using this technique (Frank et al. 2014; Kavanagh et al. 2009) on ground and surface waters in the Athabasca region (n >300), a positive SFS signal has solely been associated with the presence of ANAs. For the purposes of this study, it was assumed there was no contribution from neutral aromatic substances, besides which they are not expected in groundwater. Due to unknown contributions with maxima at and beyond 340 nm, potentially from humic acids, elevated intensities were observed at 333 without distinct maxima at this wavelength nor at 282 and 320 nm (Figure S5). Consequently comparisons at 333 nm have been excluded from this analysis.

Statistical Analyses

The application of the various statistical analyses to this data set is shown in Table 1. The Mood's median test (sign score test; Minitab 2010) was used to test the significance of any difference in parameters between pond-site and non-pond sample sets (Assessment 1) because the data for most variables were non-normally distributed, even after log-transformation, and were influenced by outlier values. To assess concentration-depth trends in multiple-depth data sets (Assessment 2), differences in parameter values between samples from two consecutive depths were calculated and divided by the difference in depth (in cm). These concentration-difference-per-depth-difference values were assessed with boxplots of 95% confidence intervals for the median. Overlap of the median confidence interval with zero, indicating no significant influence of sample depth, was typically observed.

PCA (Minitab 2010) was conducted on the correlation matrix of log-transformed data to assess joint variability of groundwater parameters among sites and zones (Figure 1), with separate analyses considering (1) inorganic compounds only (Assessment 3) and (2) a set of indicator parameters (Assessment 4). While the transformed data were approximately normally distributed, nonmetric multidimensional scaling (NMDS; Minitab 2010) was performed on these same data sets as a check on the PCA results.

Results and Discussion

Pond-Site vs. Non-Pond Groundwater Comparison

Groundwater samples adjacent to the study tailings pond (i.e., pond-site samples; zones PA, PB, PC, PD [Figure 1]) were compared with those collected away from any tailings pond (i.e., non-pond samples; zones EL, SB, NW, SW, NE [Figure 1]) for 29 water quality parameters (Assessment 1, Table 1). There were 68 total pond-site samples, though only 57 and 56 were analyzed for NH4 and ANA, respectively. Non-pond samples totaled 57, with 44 and 34 analyzed for NH4 and ANA, respectively. These discrepancies were largely due to the later availability of NH4 and ANA analyses. The parameters were grouped as (1) guideline compounds (20 compounds with water quality guideline concentrations for toxicity to aquatic life [CCME 2007]), (2) major ions (noting Cl is in the guideline compounds group), and (3) additional markers (see Table S4). An evaluation of the potential influence of river infiltration (hyporheic flow, bank storage) on these shallow groundwater samples is provided in Appendix S3. It suggests no substantial influence for the vast majority of samples, with no apparent bias between pond-site and non-pond samples.

Sampling Depth Assessment

Groundwater was generally collected at shallower depths below the sediment surface for non-pond (mean 56 cm) vs. pond-site (mean 94 cm) samples. This difference in sampling depth is attributed mainly to the presence of limestone limiting penetration depth or fine sediments at depth limiting water withdrawal, at many of the non-pond locations, whereas much of the pond area had thick sand deposits. Consideration of the 32 multiple-depth sample combinations (Assessment 2, Table 1) indicates that most of the parameters exhibited no significant differences with depth (i.e., the confidence interval of the median change in concentration with change in depth bounded zero). Those that increased with depth were Ag, Be, Br, Fe, NH4, and Se. Many of these (Fe, NH4) are commonly elevated under reducing conditions, which may be more likely further from the streambed interface and its comparatively high dissolved oxygen levels (data not shown). For these parameters, any findings of higher levels at pond-site locations (which tend to be sampled at greater depths) cannot discount this depth relationship as a cause. There is no indication of major ions increasing with depth, as might be expected for dilution with river water from small-scale hyporheic exchange or bank storage. Parameters that decreased with depth were Cd, Cu, Sb, Tl, and U.

Pond-Site vs. Non-Pond Risk Profile Assessment

For shallow groundwater of the pond-site data set, 11 of the 20 guideline compounds had at least one sample with concentrations above their aquatic life water quality guideline value (CCME 2007) (Table S4). Similarly though, 10 of these same 11 (minus Mo), plus one additional compound (Cu), were at concentrations above guidelines for one or more samples from the non-pond sample set. Note that elevated concentrations for the non-pond sample set spanned many different samples from several zones, indicating these results are not biased by just a few outlier samples. In addition, there appears to be no effect on these results of potential divergent flow of OSPW-affected groundwater from the study pond into the adjacent non-pond zones. Non-pond sample locations nearest to the study pond, one to the north (NW zone) and one to the south (SW zone), had only six and one compound, respectively, that were within the upper quartile for samples in their zone. Only one compound (Fe) was at the maximum (NW zone sample). A comparison of concentrations between the pond-site and non-pond sample data sets reveals similar averages and ranges (Figure 2; Table S4), with a significant difference (P < 0.05) found for only seven guideline compounds: Cr and Fe were greater for non-pond locations, while F, Mo, Ni, Se, and U were greater for pond-site locations (Ni and U were below water quality guidelines), and thus, may possibly represent the influence of OSPW-affected groundwater. Of these seven compounds, only the elevated Se for pond-site locations may be complicated by the depth-dependent relationship noted above. Thus, differences in the guideline compound concentrations are few and partly balanced between pond-site and non-pond areas.

Of the parameters commonly touted as indicators of OSPW-affected groundwater that were tested here (Figure 3, Table S4), only the Na-Cl ratio showed significantly elevated values for pond-site vs. non-pond samples. This may be a sign of groundwater impacted by OSPW from the study tailings pond, though it may also result from naturally derived variation in the groundwater systems in this area. There were no significant differences between sites for both of the SFS-ANA parameters, or for many of the other compounds, including B (Figure 2), NH4, Na, and SO4 (Figure 3).

Finally, pond and non-pond sample sets were also generally similar with respect to major ion chemistry (Table S4), especially for Ca, Mg, K, and HCO3, with more variance (higher concentrations) in Na and Cl; this suggests that the shallow groundwater samples in both were largely supplied by similar (or the same) geological formations and types of groundwater flow systems, with only a few indications of deep (more saline) groundwater influence for some non-pond samples.

The results of this pond-site vs. non-pond assessment suggest that, generally, the water quality of shallow groundwater beside the study pond is not significantly different than groundwater away from this and other tailings ponds (noting that neither appears strongly affected by infiltrating river water [Appendix S3]). This implies that few of the pond-site groundwater samples have been impacted by OSPW, as would fit the general lack of difference for most of the parameters considered in this study. This could reflect limited leakage of OSPW or deeper locations of discharge to the Athabasca River or the attenuation of metals and ANA in the groundwater system. However, the finding of this assessment is also consistent with a scenario in which pond-site samples have been affected by OSPW (perhaps even broadly), but any increases in the assessed parameter values have not boosted these above common levels found for other groundwater in the general area. For instance, Drive-points 4 and 5, representing pond-site samples, reported to be likely affected by OSPW (Frank et al. 2014), had only one guideline compound (F) with concentrations above the maximum concentration found for the non-pond samples, and only two (F and As [one sample only]) with values greater than two standard deviations above the mean of non-pond samples. Concentrations for many additional compounds (B, Mo, and V; Fe and Zn for only one sample) were above the non-pond samples mean, while those of the remaining 13 guideline compounds were below. Thus, these two samples do not stand out as extreme for this guideline parameter set, though both had elevated SFS-ANA signatures. It is not clear which of the two cases (i.e., minimal leakage vs. minor concentration differences) represents the true condition here. Regardless, in the context of relative chemical concentrations and exceedances of Canadian aquatic life guidelines (CCME 2007) for those compounds measured in this study, there appear to be no indications, at this time and over this broad scale, of an increased toxicity risk posed by the discharge of shallow riparian groundwater to the Athabasca River beside the study tailings pond. Conditions may differ, however, in the winter given the much lower river levels (Figure S1). Toxicity risks from compounds not measured here remain unknown. Also, localized contamination of groundwater cannot be ruled out by this broad comparison; indeed it has been suggested for locations of Drive-points 4 and 5 (Frank et al. 2014), and will be investigated further in the following section.

Zone-Based Assessments

In Assessments 3 and 4 (Table 1), multivariate statistics were applied to a larger set of samples that were segregated into small-scale zones (Figure 1). The study tailings pond area (i.e., pond-site samples) was split into four zones (PA, PB, PC, and PD). The majority of the other zones are away from tailings ponds (i.e., non-pond samples), though a few of them (e.g., EP, MR; not included in Assessment 1) include samples that were taken near other tailings ponds. This sample set included five drive-point groundwater samples previously addressed (Frank et al. 2014). Drive-points 1 and 3 are non-pond samples in zone SW, shown not to be affected by OSPW. Drive-points 4 (zone PA) and 5 (zone PB) have been identified as likely OSPW-affected samples, while Drive-point 6 (zone PA) was determined to be an unaffected sample despite being collected beside the study pond. Also included in the assessments were two OSPW samples previously collected from two nearby tailings ponds (Frank et al. 2014). The main premise of these analyses was that any OSPW-specific parameters would align in ordination space with the two OSPW-affected groundwater samples and the two OSPW samples, but not with samples known to be unaffected by OSPW (Drive-points 1, 3, and 6, and all other non-pond samples). Assessment 3 also contains five samples of river water (including Athabasca, Muskeg, and Steepbank Rivers).

Results of these assessments are shown graphically in Figures 4-6 as biplots of the PCA, with supporting analyses mentioned in the text only. PCA is effectively a transformation of the data, whereby the variances carried by many original parameters are reallocated among fewer new component variables (orthogonal and uncorrelated). When these new components are plotted against each other, the relationship of each original parameter is illustrated as a vector. The longer and more parallel a parameter vector is with a component axis, the more strongly related the variable is to that component. Individual data points can then be plotted within this new ordination space, with their position indicative of which parameters (vectors) are most influential. Data with similar overall parameter influences will tend to plot more closely together.

The two OSPW samples plot together in all of the PCA plots (Figures 4-6), indicating similar water chemistry, giving some confidence that variability in OSPW between different ponds may not be that great for the parameters considered here. However, OSPW water chemistry may differ in time, with older OSPW potentially being more dilute during times of less-extreme water recycling. Thus, these OSPW samples may not represent perfectly the now-reclaimed study tailings pond or any related OSPW-affected groundwater.

Zone Assessment Using Inorganic Geochemicals

The first zone-focused assessment (Table 1) contains 32 inorganic geochemicals, largely trace metals and major ions. The PCA results for this data set are shown in Figure 4, considering the first two principal components (additional components were deemed insignificant), which account for 26 and 17% of the total variance, respectively. Axis 1 is relatively parallel to the vectors for the major cations and chloride (Figure 4a), thus representing the influence of total dissolved solids (TDS), and with trace metals such as B, Cd, Sr, and Co. Axis 2 runs parallel to the vectors for redox-sensitive species Fe and SO4, as well as various other trace metals. There are few trace metals not aligned with these two axes, which indicates that TDS and redox conditions are the primary controls on these inorganic species. Possible exceptions include Se, F, and Mo, which were identified as significantly higher in pond-site compared to that of non-pond samples; their vectors are in line with the two OSPW samples, suggesting these deserve consideration as OSPW-specific indicators. Of note, F has been considered as a possible OSPW indicator parameter by industry (Jon Fennell, Integrated Sustainability, personal communication).

All five river waters congregate together in this ordination space (Figure 4), with low TDS (negative values of axis 1) and low values of both Fe and SO4 (neutral values of axis 2). They are separated from most of the groundwater samples, largely along axis 1 (i.e., they are more dilute waters). This is in line with the general conclusion from the evaluation in Appendix S3 that the vast majority of groundwater samples do not appear to be strongly influenced by river water infiltration.

The relative grouping of samples by zone via PCA (Figure 4b; the general pattern is similar for the NMDS analysis, not shown) shows that there is much overlap among zones, with many zones showing considerable spread within their individual data sets. The four pond-site zones are not clearly separated from the others. Thus, these analyses support the general finding from the pond-site vs. non-pond assessment of no major difference in guideline compound or major ion levels.

The PCA (Figure 4) does reveal some variation in groundwater inorganic geochemistry at a smaller scale than the pond-site vs. non-pond assessment. Separation of zones is somewhat more pronounced along axis 1, indicating differences in TDS. This may signify differences in geology (e.g., higher TDS from Cretaceous and Devonian formations [Gibson et al. 2013]) or groundwater residence times, or even large-scale (e.g., inter-meander) hyporheic flow influences, for samples at negative values of axis 1, near the river samples. It may also reveal an OSPW impact at higher values of axis 1, as the two OSPW samples plot in the upper right quadrant. Ross et al. (2012) also reported OSPW samples aligning with higher TDS, though in a PCA of largely surface water samples from the oil sands area. The upper right quadrant (Figure 4) also contains many PB samples, but also with some from the SW, NW, EP, and PA zones. Thus, while the presence of OSPW-affected groundwater is not proven or even strongly suggested from these results, they do imply that any OSPW influence is more likely within the PA and PB zones than that of PC and PD.

The relative positions within the PCA plots (Figure 4) of the five drive-point samples may reveal further insight into any OSPW influence. Drive-points 3 (non-pond, SW zone) and 6 (PA zone, but not influenced by OSPW) plot in the lower left quadrant, directly opposite and well-separated from the OSPW samples. The two OSPW-affected samples, Drive-points 4 (PA zone) and 5 (PB zone), are closer to the OSPW samples. However, they are not notably closer than Drive-point 1 (non-pond, SW zone), nor are they clearly separated from the range of other non-pond samples (e.g., NW and SW zones). This suggests that this broad set of inorganic geochemicals, encompassing many of the metals that tend to receive preferential attention for toxicity in aquatic ecosystems (e.g., Ag, Al, Cd, Cr, Cu, Ni, Pb, Zn), including in the oil sands region (Kelly et al. 2010), is not strongly discriminatory for OSPW effects on groundwater.

Zone Assessment Using Indicator Parameters

The second zone-focused assessment incorporates 16 parameters (Assessment 4, Table 1), including many that have been proposed as indicators for OSPW impacts on groundwater in previous studies (e.g. Na, Na-Cl ratio, NA by Baker (Baker 1999); B, NH4, NA, by MacKinnon et al. (2005); SO4, Cl, NH4 by Allen (2008); HCO3, Na, Cl, SO4, NA, B by Oiffer et al. (2009); Na, Cl by Yasuda et al. (2010); or this work (F, Mo, Se, NaCl ratio). Here, SFS-ANA 282nm and SFS-ANA 320nm (marker parameters) are proxies for NA. Also included are a few key compounds (e.g., Cl, Fe) representing controlling factors from the previous PCA (Figure 4). This assessment considered only 111 groundwater samples because samples without NH4 and SFS analyses (i.e., those from 2009 and including all from the Steepbank River [SB] zone), must be omitted from the multivariate analyses.

The first three components of this PCA account for 33, 19, and 13% of the total variation, respectively, of the indicator data set. The results (e.g., Figures 5 and 6) are fully supported by NMDS (not shown). Similar to the inorganic PCA (Figure 4), the parameters form two general groupings that are relatively perpendicular to each other (Figure 5a). One tight group consists of redox-related species (e.g., Fe, As, V, NH4). The other group has a greater spread and roughly two parts, with one consisting of (longer vectors only) SFS-ANA 282 nm, B, Na, and Na-Cl ratio, and the other of SFS-ANA 320 nm, Cl, F, and Se. The vectors for this latter part align best with the two OSPW samples and Drive-point 4 (PA zone), with Drive-point 5 (PB zone) in the same general area; this distinguishes these parameters in particular as promising indicators for OSPW-affected groundwater.

Principle component 1 is positively aligned with high SFS-ANA values (Figure 5a), which are indicative of bitumen influence on groundwater, surface water, and OSPW (Frank et al. 2014; Kavanagh et al. 2009). The two OSPW samples and likely OSPW-affected Drive-points 4 and 5 all plot at high values of axis 1, indicating similarities in these indicator parameters. Other samples with positive component 1 values are predominately from zones PA, PB, NW, and SW (Figure 5b). For pond-site zones PA and PB, this may indicate the presence of OSPW in some of these groundwater samples. However, these four zones are all along the west bank of the Athabasca River, in the same general area where outcrops of the lower McMurray formation containing natural oil sands are present. Therefore, this may also be derived from the local geology. Zones that are near other tailings ponds, such as EP and MR, are not represented in this group of samples, suggesting no OSPW influence, although this assumes that OSPW characteristics for those compounds assessed in this study do not differ substantially between ponds and over time.

Component 1 of this PCA shows some promise of distinguishing OSPW-affected groundwater, but there remains some overlap with non-pond samples in the plot of the first two components (Figure 5b), including Drive-point 1. The second component does not provide separation of the different sample types. However, considering the third component relative to Component 1 (Figure 6), a noticeable grouping of more than 14 samples in the bottom-right quadrant is observed, with Drive-points 4 and 5 the most prominent (i.e., greatest distance from centroid). This grouping is aligned with parameters F, Mo, Na-Cl ratio, and SFS-ANA 282 and 320, all of which have been identified as possible indicators in this and the preceding assessments. This grouping consists predominantly of zone PB samples, but with several zone PA samples as well. Most importantly, all of the non-pond samples, including those with positive component 1 values such as Drive-point 1, are strongly separated from this grouping. In this plot, the OSPW samples are less well-aligned with Drive-points 4 and 5 compared to Figure 5, being more neutral with respect to Component 3. However, some geochemical differences between these two OSPW samples and OSPW-affected groundwater would not be unexpected given the different source of OSPW (in space and time), mixing of different waters and the various reactions that can alter groundwater geochemistry along a flow path. It is difficult to predict the exact changes that may occur; for example, Holden et al. (2011) showed that sediments may attenuate the high Na of OSPW, but that Ca-Mg carbonate mineral formation with dissolution of sulfate salts may also act to keep Na concentrations elevated vs. background.

A closer look at the grouping identified above (Figure 6) reveals some patterns between the more than 14 samples (all from the 2010 and 2011 campaigns; 2009 samples had no SFS analysis). Six of these are paired samples from different depths. Thus, it is not surprising that they plot together in this PCA. In addition, there are three sets of two sample locations that are within several meters of each other but from different years, including one set with Drive-point 5. This suggests temporal stability in groundwater flow and chemistry patterns, though this study was not designed to address temporal patterns. This leaves nine distinct sites representing the samples in this grouping (including Drive-points 4 and 5), for which there is no evident pattern. For example, they are not predominantly congregated around the locations of Drive-points 4 and/or 5, or one or two other areas. Often there are locations of several PA or PB samples that are not in this grouping, separating the distinct sites. The mean EC of this grouping was 1960 S/cm (range of 465 to 3170 S/cm), while the mean for all samples from zones PA and PB was the same (1944; range of 270 to 5340 S/cm); thus, there is no evidence that river water interactions have influenced this group of locations less than the others in these zones. This assessment cannot determine definitively whether any of the samples of this grouping have been affected by OSPW. It is certainly possible, given variability in OSPW composition over the years and natural variability in groundwater geochemistry, that some or even all of the samples in this grouping have not been affected by OSPW or that samples that lie outside the grouping may have been affected to some degree. However, these results indicate that there may be additional, small areas (restricted to the PA, PB zones) where shallow groundwater affected by OSPW, in a similar way as has been suggested for Drive-points 4 and 5 (Frank et al. 2014), may be reaching the river sediments, requiring additional study.

Despite the uncertainty discussed above, this indicator-focused PCA suggests a possible screening methodology to identify candidate groundwater samples for more advanced (e.g., Level 2, as performed by Frank et al. [2014]) analytical testing. The idea for screening with various standard parameters is not new, but previous studies did not test such a large groundwater data set, include as wide a spectrum of potential indicators, and were unable to utilize samples with established presence/absence of OSPW influence. The key indicators appear to be the SFS-ANA parameters (though direct analysis of total NAs should suffice), along with F, Mo, Se, and Na-Cl ratio. The inclusion of SFS is obvious, given that NAs are key components of OSPW (Clemente and Fedorak 2005; MacKinnon and Boerger 1986), but are not expected at high levels for natural groundwater that has not been in contact with in situ bitumen (Gibson et al. 2013). Elevated Na-Cl ratios in OSPW compared to natural groundwaters may result from the application of Na during caustic addition (Schramm et al. 2000). The potential cause of elevated Mo, F, and Se is less certain, especially because information on the historical use of chemicals at this site is not readily available. However, Mo has been tested for use in catalysts for hydrodesulfurization of bitumen (Sharma and Olson 1995) and Mo catalysts are used in the catalytic conversion process of bitumen refinement (cracking), while F (along with B) has been used for superacid catalyzed hydrocracking of bitumen (Strausz et al. 1999). Whether these processes have influenced tailings pond chemistry here is not known. Alternately, these compounds may be naturally elevated in the oil sands formation or may be released through reactions with the dyke or aquifer materials. These same indicators may not hold true at other sites but this study can provide direction to future assessments. Note that F, Se, and Mo were generally reported at similar concentrations in OSPW from other tailings ponds (Gibson et al. 2011) as for the two OSPW samples included in this assessment.

Conclusions

The first part of this study consisted of a comparison of concentrations of geochemical constituents (or proxies), including trace metals or metalloids, major ions, and fluorescence profiles of ANAs, in shallow groundwater from riparian areas adjacent to a tailings pond (i.e., pond-site samples) and those away from any tailings pond (i.e., non-pond samples). The underlying hypothesis here is that a higher risk profile adjacent to the study pond, which is one of the first tailings ponds created in the area and is the closest to the Athabasca River, would be associated with OSPW contamination. For both data sets (125 samples total), 11 of the 20 guideline compounds had at least one sample with concentrations above their aquatic life water quality guideline value. However, with only a few exceptions (F, Mo, Ni, Se, and U), the concentrations of these guideline compounds were not significantly greater for the pond-site sample set. Meanwhile, Cr and Fe were greater for non-pond locations. This suggests that, for the tested constituents and at the broad scale considered here, shallow groundwater conditions adjacent to the study pond currently pose no greater toxicity risk to benthic and other aquatic life than nearby reference sites. There is potential that these shallow groundwater samples have been influenced to some degree by river water infiltration (e.g., hyporheic flow, bank storage), though an evaluation considering river hydrographs and comparing river water and groundwater chemistry suggests there is no substantial influence for the vast majority of samples. The potential effects from deeper groundwater flow paths discharging to the middle of the river, rather than the shallow groundwater discharge along the banks, are not considered here. These results may not represent conditions during winter, under much reduced river levels.

For the second part of the study, a larger portion of the groundwater data set (150 samples) was separated into 11 smaller zones (some near a pond, some not) and subjected to multivariate analyses to determine whether any combinations of standard geochemistry metrics could cost-effectively screen large groundwater sample sets prior to more advanced analytical confirmation of OSPW contamination. This data set included five samples previously determined as likely affected or not affected by OSPW (Frank et al. 2014); in addition, two OSPW samples were also included in these analyses. Geological and potential OSPW influences could not be distinguished based on major ions and metal/metalloid concentrations, because there was substantial overlap of data for pond and non-pond zones. This supports the findings from the first part of the study, but also suggests that neither data set was unduly influenced by deeper-sourced, brine-impacted groundwater or by infiltration of low-EC river water. However, for several indicator parameters (SFS-ANA, F, Mo, Se, and Na-Cl ratio) similarities between a small subset of samples from two study pond zones and the two OSPW samples and the two groundwater samples documented as likely OSPW-affected were noted. This indicates that OSPW-affected groundwater may be reaching Athabasca River sediments at a few additional locations than suggested previously (Frank et al. 2014). It also provides some suggestions for screening parameters for OSPW-affected groundwater, though additional study is needed.
